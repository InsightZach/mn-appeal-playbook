# Worked Example — 15012 Cherry Ln, Minnetonka (Hennepin County)

The win — and the one that proves the thesis. This parcel had only a **borderline** angle on paper, weaker
than the Minneapolis example. It got a **reduction** anyway, because a city appraiser inspected the
property, corrected the record, and agreed to a revised value. Engagement, not the packet, produced the
result.

## Triage

**Verdict: borderline.** The script flagged a best comp (15124 Lynn Ter) that sold ~13.6% below the
subject's EMV — but on judgment that comp's *own* assessment already matched its sale price, so it
reflected a different value tier, not assessment inequity. With no published finished-SF for the subject
(a suburban Hennepin data gap), the $/SF and equalization analyses could not run. On paper this was a
weaker case than the Minneapolis parcel — a marginal angle resting mainly on the +15.1% 2025 jump.

## How it was run — and why it worked

The appeal reached Minnetonka late (received the final day of the Local Board), and the city added it to
the agenda as "no change" to let it advance. But Minnetonka, unlike Minneapolis, runs **open-book
appraiser review with an inspection**. The city appraiser **inspected the property**, and — **after
correcting the property record** — agreed with the owner on a revised value.

## Outcome

**Reduced: $952,200 → $922,700** (−$29,500, −3.1%), finalized at the Hennepin County Board. The reduction
came from the **appraiser correcting property data on inspection**, not from the packet's comparable-sales
argument.

> A note on follow-through: the appraiser also phoned and emailed the filer with the findings and, on the
> other Minnetonka parcel in the batch, got no response — and that one closed at no change. The inspection
> is necessary but not sufficient; someone has to **own the appraiser's follow-up** and carry the
> negotiation. See [Inspections](../docs/07-inspections.md).

## The lesson

A weaker case in an open-book-with-inspection jurisdiction beat a stronger case in a no-inspection
jurisdiction — because the process put an appraiser on the property and someone corrected the record with
them. That is the entire argument for running appeals as an operation: route the parcel to the right
process, get onto the property, and follow through.
