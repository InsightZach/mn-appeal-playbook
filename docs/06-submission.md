# 6. Submitting the Appeal — and Why Not to Mail It

How an appeal is submitted matters as much as what it says. The packet is **evidence for a conversation**,
not a substitute for one. The goal of submission is to get the packet in front of the appraiser *and* to
open the dialogue — ideally during the open-book window, where the appraiser still has discretion to
adjust the value.

## The core principle

> A mailed packet with no human engagement is heard "as filed." An engaged appeal — packet plus a
> conversation plus, where the jurisdiction allows, an inspection — gives the appraiser the means and the
> motive to change the number.

## Why mailing a packet to the board is the wrong move

Mailing a written appeal directly to the board, with no appraiser contact, fails for structural reasons:

1. **It skips the open-book window.** Open book is the one stage where an appraiser can change the value
   unilaterally, before any position has hardened. A mailed board appeal arrives *after* that
   opportunity, at the formal stage.
2. **The board defers to staff who never engaged.** A local or county board leans on the assessing
   department's recommendation. If no appraiser reviewed the property or spoke with anyone, the
   department's recommendation is "no change," and the board adopts it.
3. **No inspection gets triggered.** In jurisdictions where appeals can prompt an inspection, that happens
   through engagement, not through a mailed letter. No inspection means no chance to correct the property
   record — usually the strongest argument.
4. **A packet alone cannot answer questions.** The appraiser will have questions about your comps or
   corrections. A letter cannot answer them; a person can.

### What the 26p27 cycle showed

The contrast is concrete. Across one batch of Minnesota residential appeals:

- **Minneapolis (written appeals to the Local Board, no engagement):** the assessing department presented
  no information, no inspections were conducted, and the board **sustained every value — no change.** The
  appeals were heard exactly as mailed.
- **Minnetonka (city appraiser engaged and inspected):** the appraiser inspected the property, corrected
  the property record, and **agreed to a reduced value** that was finalized at the county board.

Same kind of property, same season, opposite outcomes — driven entirely by whether anyone engaged the
appraiser and got onto the property.

## How to submit, by stage

| Stage | How to submit | Why |
|-------|---------------|-----|
| **Open book / informal review** | Contact the assessor's office directly (the channel on the notice); deliver the packet to the reviewing appraiser; request a review and, where applicable, an inspection | This is where discretion lives — prioritize it |
| **Local Board of Appeal & Equalization** | Appear (in person, by phone, or by the jurisdiction's stated method) by the local board date; submit the packet as the board record | Required step before the county board |
| **County Board of Appeal & Equalization** | Submit the request to be heard by the county's deadline (often early May); present at the June hearing | The escalation path when open book / local board did not resolve it |
| **Tax Court** | File the petition by April 30 of the payable year | The formal backstop (see [Negotiation & Escalation](08-negotiation-escalation.md)) |

> Submitting a packet to the board is not wrong **as the record for a hearing you are attending**. It is
> wrong as a *replacement* for engaging the appraiser. Submit the packet **and** show up.

## Authorization

Assessor offices will generally only discuss parcel-specific appeal information with the **owner** or a
representative the owner has **authorized in writing**. Have a signed owner authorization (Letter of
Authorization) on file for each parcel before you call — otherwise the first conversation stalls on
"who are you to ask." Build authorization collection into onboarding, not into the appeal scramble.
