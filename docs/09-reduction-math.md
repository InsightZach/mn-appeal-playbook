# 9. Calculating the Reduction

A value reduction only matters as **tax savings**. The client cares about the dollars off the tax bill,
and under a contingency model the fee is a share of those dollars. This chapter covers how to convert an
EMV reduction into a savings estimate — and how to do it before the payable-year tax rate is even
published.

## The basic relationship

```
estimated tax savings  ≈  EMV reduction  ×  effective tax rate (ETR)
```

where

```
ETR  =  total property tax  ÷  estimated market value (EMV)
```

The ETR expresses the property's tax as a fraction of its assessed value. Multiply the EMV reduction you
achieve by the ETR and you have the approximate annual tax savings.

## The timing problem — and the proxy

The payable-year tax rate is **not set when you are appealing**. Local levies are certified and rates are
calculated late in the assessment year and into the payable year. During the spring–summer appeal season
you do **not** yet know the rate that will apply to the value you are contesting.

The fix: **use the most recent fully-known ETR as a proxy.**

| Cycle you are appealing | ETR proxy to use | Why |
|-------------------------|------------------|-----|
| **26p27** (appealed in 2026) | **25p26 ETR** | The 26p27 (payable-2027) rate is not published until early 2027 |
| **27p28** (appealed in 2027) | **26p27 ETR** | The 27p28 (payable-2028) rate is not published until early 2028 |

In general: when appraising the savings on assessment year *N* (payable *N+1*), use the ETR from year
*N−1* (payable *N*), which is the most recent rate already calculated. Rates move slowly year to year, so
the prior-year ETR is a sound estimate for prioritization and client savings figures.

## Worked example

A Ramsey home assessed at $511,900 with a known prior-year total tax of about $9,100:

```
ETR (proxy)     = $9,100 / $511,900            ≈ 1.78%
EMV reduction   = $511,900 − $470,000          = $41,900
estimated savings = $41,900 × 1.78%            ≈ $746 / year
```

If the reduction holds for more than one year, multiply across the years it is expected to hold (a
stipulated value generally holds at least into the following year; an open-book concession may not — see
[Negotiation & Escalation](08-negotiation-escalation.md)).

## Caveats — label it an estimate

The raw ETR is an approximation for residential property, because:

- **Homestead market value exclusion** reduces the taxable value for owner-occupied homes, so the
  *effective* rate on EMV is not the published tax rate.
- **Class rates** differ by property type, so a non-homestead or mixed-use parcel carries a different
  effective rate.
- **Levy changes** between years shift the rate slightly.

For triage and client savings estimates, the prior-year-ETR proxy is accurate enough — just present it as
an **estimate**, not a guaranteed figure. When the payable-year rate is published, the actual savings can
be restated.

## Why this feeds triage

The savings estimate is what makes the [worth-it decision](04-triage-decision.md) real: a 3% EMV
reduction on a modestly assessed home may not clear the **~$1,000/yr minimum client savings**, while the
same percentage on a high-value parcel can. But it is the **dollar savings** (reduction × ETR), not the EMV
or the %, that decides — a thin reduction on a high-value parcel can still fall short. Run the ETR math at
triage, not after the work — it tells you which parcels deserve a full packet and which do not.
