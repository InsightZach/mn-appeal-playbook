# 2. The Jurisdiction Map

Before you can appeal a parcel, you have to know **who assesses it** and **what that body's appeal format
and windows are**. In Minnesota this is not uniform — and getting it wrong means submitting the right
argument to the wrong office, in the wrong format, after the window has closed.

This chapter uses Ramsey and Hennepin to teach the two cases every multi-jurisdiction operator must
handle: the **single-authority county** and the **mixed county-plus-cities county**.

## How to find the assessing authority for a parcel

Two reliable methods, in order:

1. **Read the valuation notice.** It names the office that set the value and the office to contact to
   appeal, with the contact information and the local board / open book dates for that jurisdiction. The
   notice is authoritative for that parcel and year.
2. **Check the county assessor's jurisdiction list.** The county knows which cities it assesses and which
   self-assess. Confirm the current list each year — cities occasionally transfer assessment to or from
   the county.

Do not assume a parcel is assessed by the county just because it sits in the county. In Hennepin, several
cities run their own assessing departments.

## Ramsey County — the single-authority case

Ramsey County assesses **every city and township in the county**. There is one process, one set of
windows, and one contact surface.

```
All Ramsey parcels
   → Ramsey County Assessor (open book / informal review)
   → Ramsey County Local Board of Appeal & Equalization  (or open book in lieu)
   → Ramsey County Special Board of Appeal & Equalization (county board)
   → Minnesota Tax Court (petition by April 30 of payable year)
```

- **One contact:** the Ramsey County Assessor's office. (See [contacts](../contacts/).)
- **One calendar:** the county's published open-book and board dates apply countywide.
- **Routing logic:** trivial — every parcel goes to the same place.

Ramsey is the clean case. It is what every jurisdiction would look like if assessment were centralized.

## Hennepin County — the mixed case

Hennepin County assesses most of its suburbs, but **several cities maintain their own assessing
departments** and run their own local board or open-book process. All of them, county-assessed or
self-assessed, funnel up to the **Hennepin County Board of Appeal & Equalization**.

```
Hennepin parcel
   ├─ City self-assesses?  → City Assessor (city's open book / local board format)
   └─ County assesses?     → Hennepin County Assessor (open book / local board)
                                   ↓ (both paths)
                        Hennepin County Board of Appeal & Equalization
                                   ↓
                        Minnesota Tax Court (petition by April 30 of payable year)
```

Cities that have historically self-assessed include **Minneapolis, Minnetonka, Bloomington, Edina, St.
Louis Park**, and others. **Confirm the current list with Hennepin County each year** — do not hard-code
it. The valuation notice resolves it per parcel.

### The two formats, illustrated — Minneapolis vs. Minnetonka

These two self-assessing cities handle appeals very differently, which is exactly why jurisdiction
routing matters:

| | City of Minneapolis | City of Minnetonka |
|---|---|---|
| Assessing body | City of Minneapolis Assessing | City of Minnetonka Assessing |
| Open-book / informal review | Limited | Yes — active appraiser review |
| Inspection on appeal | **No** — written appeals to the Local Board are decided without an inspection or department presentation (city policy) | **Yes** — city appraiser inspects the property |
| Practical consequence | A written appeal with no engagement is heard "as filed" and typically sustained | An inspection lets the appraiser correct property data and adjust the value |
| Escalation | Local Board → Hennepin County Board | Local Board → Hennepin County Board |

The lesson is not "Minneapolis is harder." It is that **the same packet, submitted the same way, produces
different outcomes in different jurisdictions** — and you cannot run an appeal program without knowing
which format applies to each parcel.

### What this means operationally

For each parcel on the list, before the season starts, record:

- **Assessing authority** (county or which city)
- **Appeal format** (open book vs. local board; does the format include an inspection?)
- **Windows** (open book dates, local board date, county board request deadline) — from the notice
- **Contact** (office, appraiser if known, email, phone)

This is the per-parcel routing table that drives everything downstream. A parcel in a no-inspection
written-appeal jurisdiction needs a different plan (engage *before* the local board, or plan straight for
the county board / Tax Court) than a parcel in an open-book-with-inspection jurisdiction (where the win is
getting the appraiser to the property).
