# The Property Record Card (CAMA Inputs) — Correct the Model, Not Just the Comps

> Cross-cutting method. It starts as a **data step** (request the card — chapter [3](03-data-sources.md)),
> drives the **packet** (chapter [5](05-packet-generation.md)), and is what an **inspection** (chapter
> [7](07-inspections.md)) exists to document. For residential it is the **first lever**, not an afterthought.

## Why this is the residential appeal's first lever

A residential assessment is not an appraiser's hand-built opinion — it is the output of a **CAMA mass-appraisal
model**. The value is computed from a set of inputs the assessor holds on the property record (a.k.a. field
card / property card):

- **Quality / grade** (e.g. a 7.5 vs 7 construction grade)
- **Condition / CDU** (Excellent → Good → Average → Fair → Poor)
- **Gross living area / above-grade SF**
- **Basement** — finished vs. unfinished, and percent finished
- **Bath count, bedroom count, effective year built / age**
- **Land line** (lot size, influence factors, view/water codes)
- **Outbuildings, garages, decks, amenities**

If an input is wrong, the value is wrong. **Correcting the input re-rates the value at its source** — which is
usually a higher-yield move than arguing a sale-plus-adjustment, for three reasons:

1. **It's an objective record correction, not a matter of judgment.** The appraiser can change a condition or
   grade code unilaterally without conceding their read of the market. "Your card says Average; here are photos
   showing Fair" is far easier to grant than "my three comps minus adjustments say your value is too high."
2. **It compounds.** A grade or condition change flows through the model — it re-prices the subject against the
   whole comparable population, not just one line.
3. **It meets less resistance.** Assessors defend their market judgment; they will fix a documented data error.

In practice the most reliable reductions come from record corrections caught at the open-book / inspection
stage — see the Minnetonka example in [Submission](06-submission.md), where the reduction came from the
appraiser **correcting the property record on inspection**, not from the sales comps.

## Step 1: get the card — make it a standing request

Pull the property record card at the **start** of the process, before you commit to a method — it tells you
which lever exists before you spend effort building a sales grid that may not be the strongest argument. Build
a **templated records request to the assessing authority** into onboarding, one per parcel.

What's available, by source (MN examples):

| Jurisdiction | Online | The qualitative grades (quality / CDU) |
|---|---|---|
| **Hennepin (county-assessed)** | Much of the record is online via property info (PINS) | Often **not** fully online — request the full card from the county assessor |
| **Self-assessing cities** (Minneapolis, etc.) | City property-info portal | Request the full card from the **city** assessor |
| **Ramsey** | Public property records portal (Beacon) | Beacon carries more of the record; confirm grade/condition with the assessor if absent |

Authorization: assessors discuss parcel-specific records only with the owner or a representative **authorized
in writing** — have the owner authorization on file before you request (see [Submission](06-submission.md)).

## Reading the card — the high-yield inputs

Check each input against reality **as of January 2 of the assessment year**, and against peer parcels' cards
(equalization of *inputs*, not just of value):

- **Condition / CDU** — the single highest-yield lever. Average → Fair is a common, defensible correction on an
  un-updated home.
- **Quality / grade** — each step moves the cost/market multiplier; a half-grade overstatement is real money.
- **Above-grade SF / GLA** — measurement or addition errors directly inflate value; verify against the sketch.
- **Basement finish** — "finished" coding (or an overstated finished %) on an unfinished or substandard
  basement is common and concrete.
- **Effective age, bath count, outbuildings, land influence codes** — each is a checkable fact.

## You still need evidence — the card says *which* lever, the evidence *pulls* it

Assessors will not drop a condition or grade on assertion alone. Pair the card review with:

- **Photos** of deferred maintenance, dated finishes, or functional problems
- **An inspection** (the highest-impact path — chapter [7](07-inspections.md))
- **Cost-to-cure / repair bids** to quantify the condition gap
- **Permit history** — the *absence* of remodel permits rebuts a high grade/condition

## Pair it with the sales approach — not either/or

The card correction is the durable lever; the sales / equalization analysis sets the market anchor and brackets
the ask. The strongest packets do both: open with the record corrections (least rebuttable), then support the
concluded value with comps. See [Packet Generation](05-packet-generation.md).
