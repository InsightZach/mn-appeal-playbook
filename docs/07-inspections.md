# 7. Inspections

In the jurisdictions that allow it, the inspection is where appeals are won. It is the appraiser's chance
to correct the property record — grade, condition, square footage, basement finish, deferred maintenance —
against what they actually see. A property-record correction is the most durable reduction available,
because it fixes the county's own data rather than arguing against it.

## When an inspection happens

It is jurisdiction-dependent (see [Jurisdiction Map](02-jurisdiction-map.md)):

- **Open-book jurisdictions with appraiser review** (e.g., Minnetonka, Ramsey County): a review or appeal
  commonly triggers an appraiser visit.
- **Written-appeal jurisdictions** (e.g., Minneapolis Local Board): by policy, written appeals are
  decided **without** an inspection. In these jurisdictions, the strategy is to engage *before* the
  written-appeal stage, or to plan for the county board / Tax Court where an independent review is
  possible.

Know which case each parcel is in before the season starts.

## Setting up the inspection

1. **Request the review** through the open-book / informal channel as early in the window as possible.
2. **Coordinate a time that works for both the appraiser and the owner.** The inspection needs interior
   access, so the owner (or a representative with access) has to be there. This is a three-way scheduling
   problem — appraiser, owner, and your team — and it is the step that most often falls apart.
3. **Confirm access and scope** — interior and exterior; make sure the appraiser can see the rooms and
   systems that support the condition argument.
4. **Prep the owner** (next section).

## Should a representative be present?

**Yes — be present, or immediately reachable, for every inspection.** The representative:

- Walks the appraiser through the condition and CAMA-error argument in person.
- Hands over the packet and points to the specific corrections.
- Answers the appraiser's questions on the spot — the questions a mailed letter cannot answer.
- Captures what the appraiser says they will do, so the follow-up is concrete.

The owner alone usually cannot make the assessment case — they know the house, not the comps or the CAMA
record. Leaving the inspection to the owner forfeits the argument at the one moment the appraiser is
standing in the property.

> **Design the follow-through, not just the visit.** The most common failure is not a missed inspection —
> it is a completed inspection with no one to receive the result. In the 26p27 cycle, a city appraiser
> inspected a property, then phoned and emailed the appeal filer with the findings and got **no response**;
> the inspection happened but the negotiation died on the handoff. Assign a named owner to each
> inspection who is reachable during and after it, and treat the appraiser's follow-up call as the most
> important call of the cycle.

## Prepping the owner

Before the appraiser arrives, brief the owner to:

- **Show** the condition issues that support the appeal: deferred maintenance, dated systems, water or
  structural problems, functional obsolescence, unfinished or substandard areas.
- **Have documentation ready** — repair estimates, inspection reports, photos of problems, permit history,
  and the property's prior listing if any (see the
  [listing enrichment guide](../collectors/listing_enrichment.md)).
- **Be factual, not adversarial.** The appraiser is doing their job; the goal is an accurate record, not a
  confrontation.
- **Not volunteer value-adding improvements** that aren't relevant to the assessment-date condition, and
  not speculate about value — let the packet and the representative carry the valuation argument.

## After the inspection

The appraiser issues findings — supported value, or a recommended adjustment. That hand-off is the start
of the negotiation, covered in [Negotiation & Escalation](08-negotiation-escalation.md). Record the
result and the procedural level (open-book concession vs. board action), because that determines both the
next step this year and the leverage next year.
